PROPOSAL: ASIAN ROVER CHALLENGE (AsRC) 2026
1. Executive Summary
The Asian Rover Challenge (AsRC) is a premier student-led Mars robotics competition
designed to prepare the next generation of engineers for the challenges of planetary
habitation and infrastructure development. Hosted in Kerala, India, and organized by RISA
(Robotic Systems Integrators Association) in collaboration with Mar Athanasius College of
Engineering (MACE), AsRC brings together engineering students from across Asia to design,
build, and operate advanced Mars-capable robotic systems in realistic mission environments.
The Paradigm Shift: Beyond Initial Exploration
Unlike traditional rover competitions that focus primarily on initial exploration and scientific
discovery, AsRC is built around a fundamentally different question: What happens after
exploration?
For radiography and decades, prestigious international competitions such as the University
Rover Challenge (URC) in the USA, the European Rover Challenge (ERC) in Poland, the
Canadian International Rover Challenge (CIRC), and the Anatolian Rover Challenge have
established excellent global standards for student planetary robotics. Their missions
successfully replicate humanity's first encounters with a planetary surface—exploring unknown
terrain, collecting geological samples, identifying scientific targets, and demonstrating
autonomous mobility.
The Asian Rover Challenge respects and builds upon this foundation but shifts the paradigm
entirely. AsRC is set in a near-future Mars, approximately in the 2040s, where the era of initial
exploration has already been completed. High-resolution maps exist, sample caches have been
deposited, and geological surveys are complete. The challenge facing humanity is no longer
discovering Mars—it is learning how to live, operate, and build there.
In this context, robotic systems must evolve beyond being mobile scientific instruments. They
must become infrastructure-enabling machines—"mobile workers"—capable of supporting
long-term human presence. The competition, therefore, introduces a new generation of
mission scenarios focused on maintenance, recovery, logistics, deployment, and construction.
Democratizing Space Robotics for Asia
While elite competitions in the United States and Europe represent the pinnacle of student
aerospace engineering, participating in them remains an inaccessible dream for the vast
majority of talented student teams across India and Asia. Prohibitive travel expenses, complex
international shipping logistics for heavy rover systems, and stringent visa barriers prevent
hundreds of brilliant young minds from showcasing their innovations on a global stage.
By hosting AsRC at MACE in Kerala, we establish a world-class, financially viable, and
logistically accessible alternative. This competition will democratize elite space robotics in the
region, offering Indian and Asian teams a high-caliber arena to test their systems without the
crippling financial burdens of Western-centric travel.
2. Objectives
The Asian Rover Challenge (AsRC) is designed to serve as a comprehensive platform for
advancing robotics, space technology, and engineering education while preparing students to
tackle the challenges of future planetary habitation.
● Promote Robotics and Space Technology Education: Increase awareness and interest in
robotics, automation, autonomous systems, and planetary exploration among students
across Asia. By exposing participants to real-world engineering challenges, the
competition encourages students to explore emerging technologies and apply theoretical
concepts in meaningful engineering applications.
● Fostering Indian and Asian Talent (Democratization): Provide an elite, highly accessible,
and affordable platform for teams from SAARC and Asian nations, eliminating the massive
logistical and financial barriers associated with traveling to Western-based competitions.
● Establish MACE’s Global Academic Repute: Position Mar Athanasius College of
Engineering as a premier pioneer in space-tech education. Hosting an international event
of this scale will draw global media attention, research collaborations, and enhance
institutional standing in international academic circles and national accreditations (such as
NBA and NAAC).
● Encourage Multidisciplinary Collaboration: Modern planetary missions require expertise
from multiple engineering domains working together. The competition encourages
collaboration among students from mechanical, electronics, electrical, computer science,
artificial intelligence, mechatronics, and civil engineering.
● Advance Infrastructure-Oriented Planetary Robotics: Lead the academic transition
from exploration-based rovers to infrastructure-oriented planetary robotics. Through
mission requirements involving autonomous navigation, robotic manipulation,
maintenance operations, and construction tasks, participants are motivated to develop
innovative solutions for sustaining extraterrestrial settlements.
● Develop Future Technology Leaders: Cultivate leadership, project management,
budgeting, and systems-engineering skills among participants, helping to train the next
generation of space and automation industry professionals.
3. Background and Need
The twenty-first century has witnessed a historic surge in global interest in space exploration.
Space agencies like ISRO, NASA, and ESA, alongside private aerospace corporations, are
actively developing technologies to support permanent human presence beyond Earth.
Planetary rovers serve as the critical vanguard of these missions, functioning as the primary
tools for construction, logistics, and maintenance in environments hazardous to humans.
However, a major gap exists in modern engineering education. While students gain strong
theoretical knowledge through coursework, they rarely have opportunities to apply concepts
from multiple disciplines in a highly integrated, high-pressure environment. Rover competitions
have emerged worldwide as one of the most effective methods for addressing this challenge,
offering unparalleled experiential learning.
Currently, existing international rover events are concentrated in the Western hemisphere,
creating a geographical and economic barrier for Asian universities. Furthermore, these
existing competitions focus almost exclusively on the early exploration phase. As humanity
prepares to establish semi-permanent bases on the Moon and Mars, there is an urgent
industrial and academic need for engineering programs to pivot toward maintenance, logistics,
and infrastructure support.
The Asian Rover Challenge directly addresses this dual need. It provides a highly challenging,
multidisciplinary engineering experience tailored specifically to the next generation of space
habitation challenges, hosted in a geographically and financially accessible location for the
eastern hemisphere.
4. Why MACE?
Mar Athanasius College of Engineering (MACE), Kothamangalam, with its legacy spanning more
than six decades of academic excellence, provides the perfect ecosystem for hosting an
international technical event of this scale.
Strong Robotics and Innovation Ecosystem (Team Srishti)
At the center of MACE’s technical culture is Team Srishti, the college’s flagship student rover
team. Having competed firsthand in prestigious international planetary rover competitions,
Team Srishti possesses deep technical and operational knowledge of rover design, systems
integration, and competition dynamics. Their practical experience ensures that MACE has the
direct expertise required to design, construct, and judge a world-class simulated Martian
arena.
Advanced Laboratories & Prototyping Facilities
MACE’s newly established Robotics and Automation Laboratory, combined with its
state-of-the-art FabLab, offers participants and organizers access to advanced prototyping,
digital fabrication, and electronics testing equipment. Extensive engineering departments
(Mechanical, Civil, Electrical, Electronics, and Computer Science) provide a robust network of
laboratories and machine shops to support participating teams with emergency repairs and
technical troubleshooting during the event.
Strategic Location and Accessibility
Situated in Kothamangalam, the "Gateway to the High Ranges," the campus offers the
expansive physical space required to build a realistic, large-scale simulated Martian terrain.
Simultaneously, the college remains highly accessible:
● Air Connectivity: Cochin International Airport (COK) is located just one hour away from
the campus, providing direct flights for national and international teams.
● Rail and Road Connectivity: Major railway hubs like Aluva and Ernakulam connect the
campus directly to all parts of India.
Collaboration with Mar Athanasius College of Arts and Science (MACAS)
By collaborating with the sister institution, MACAS, MACE can utilize expanded infrastructure,
including additional auditoriums, seminar halls, extensive dining systems, and accommodation
spaces, ensuring a seamless experience for hundreds of visiting competitors and delegates.
5. Proposed Competition Structure
The Asian Rover Challenge is organized as a rigorous, two-stage competition to evaluate both
theoretical engineering design and physical operational excellence.
Stage 1: Online Qualification Round
Teams submit a comprehensive engineering package, which is evaluated by a panel of industry
experts:
● System Design Report & Video Presentation
● Science Report & Cost Analysis
● Settlement Design Report & 3D CAD Model (Focusing on a proposed Martian settlement
layout and rover docking station).
Stage 2: On-Site Finals (3-Day Field Competition)
The top-ranked qualifying teams bring their rovers to the custom-built simulated Martian
terrain at MACE to execute high-stakes physical missions:
● Mission 1: Maintenance & Repair Operation: Restoring power and communications to a
dormant infrastructure asset by interacting with control panels.
● Mission 2: Sample Recovery & Transport: Safely retrieving and transporting cached
resource payloads across difficult terrain.
● Mission 3: Pavement Construction Task: Actively deploying interlocking tiles to
construct a traversable path over soft regolith.
● Mission 4: Science Investigation Task: Conducting an in-situ scientific study of a
designated geological anomaly.

7. Infrastructure Requirements
